There is a widespread belief that technical writing must be dry — that rigour
demands dullness. This belief is wrong, and acting on it produces prose that
obscures more than it reveals.

## The clarity paradox

<Sidenote>
  Richard Feynman was famous for demanding plain-language explanations. If you
  couldn't explain something simply, he argued, you didn't understand it yet.
</Sidenote>

Precision and clarity are not in tension. A sentence that says exactly what it
means — no more, no less — is both more rigorous and easier to read than one
hedged with qualifications and passive constructions.

The impulse toward complexity often comes from insecurity. We use jargon to
signal membership in a community, and passive voice to avoid accountability.
Neither serves the reader.

## What the reader actually needs

A reader approaching a technical document has one goal: to build a correct
mental model of something they did not previously understand. Every sentence
either advances that goal or wastes their time.

Concretely, this means:

1. **Start with the conclusion.** The reader should know what they are about to
   learn before they learn it. Front-load the key insight.

2. **Use examples early.** Abstract definitions without examples are
   memorisable but not understandable. The example is the thing; the definition
   is just a compact reference.

3. **Define terms once, then use them consistently.** Synonyms in technical
   writing create ambiguity. If you call it a "context" on page one, call it a
   "context" on page ten.

## On mathematics

Mathematical notation is the most efficient language humans have invented for
a certain class of ideas. But it requires translation.

A formula like $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}$ is not
self-explanatory to someone who has not internalised the notation. The writer's
job is to bridge the formal and the intuitive:

> This says that electric field lines can only start or end on charges. Where
> there is no charge, field lines flow through without diverging.

The mathematical statement and the prose statement are the same idea in two
registers. Use both.

## The discipline

Writing clearly about technical subjects is harder than writing obscurely. It
requires you to understand something well enough to explain it without
scaffolding — without the jargon that substitutes for understanding.

That difficulty is the point. The act of writing clearly *is* the act of
thinking clearly. A confused sentence usually indicates a confused thought.
Fixing the sentence often fixes the thought.

Write for the reader who is as smart as you but does not yet know what you know.
